Provisional Waivers – Kalamazoo Immigration Attorney Marc Asch

Keeping Families Together During the Waiver Process

Before provisional waivers were introduced in March 2013, many immigrant families were separated for extended periods. Applicants seeking a waiver had to return to their home country and apply at a U.S. consulate abroad. The process was expensive, time-consuming, and risky—if the waiver was denied, applicants could face up to 10 years away from their loved ones.

With the introduction of the I-601A provisional waiver, certain immediate relatives of U.S. citizens—spouses, children, and parents—can now apply for the waiver from within the United States. This reform significantly reduces the time families must be apart during the waiver process.

Challenges in the Provisional Waiver Process

While the reform has helped many families stay together, it has also led to a rise in denials.

USCIS officials may refuse a waiver based on a "reason to believe" that the applicant could be found inadmissible for reasons other than unlawful presence—sometimes for minor issues such as a traffic violation. Denials based on such minimal infractions can be unjust, but they do happen.

"Reason to Believe" Denials

USCIS may deny waivers based on minimal concerns about admissibility, even for minor infractions like traffic violations.

No Appeal Process

If the waiver is denied, there is no formal appeal process available to applicants.

Provisional Waivers and Removal Proceedings

The provisional waiver process is also available to individuals in removal proceedings.

In many cases, it is possible to request that the immigration court temporarily close the case to allow the applicant to file for a provisional waiver.

Case Closure

Immigration courts can temporarily close removal cases to allow provisional waiver applications.

Reapplication Options

If denied, applicants can submit new waiver applications with revised and stronger evidence.

Important: If the waiver is denied, USCIS does not automatically reinstate removal proceedings after a denial.
